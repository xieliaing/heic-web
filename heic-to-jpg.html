    Why convert HEIC to JPG?

    Since iOS 11, iPhones and iPads save photos in HEIC (High Efficiency Image Container) by default. HEIC files are roughly half the size of JPG at the same visual quality, which is great for your iCloud storage — but terrible the moment you try to share those photos with anyone not on an Apple device.

    Windows 10 and 11 can technically open HEIC files, but only after you install a paid codec from the Microsoft Store. Most photo editors, older software, content management systems, job application portals, printing services, and countless websites still reject HEIC outright. The fastest fix is to convert to JPG — the universal format that works literally everywhere.

    How HEIC Quick is different

    Nearly every "free HEIC to JPG" site works by uploading your photos to a server, converting them there, and sending them back. That means strangers' servers briefly hold your personal photos — your vacations, your family, your documents. Most of these sites also limit you to a handful of files per hour, plaster ads everywhere, and require signups.

    HEIC Quick runs the entire conversion inside your browser using a WebAssembly build of libheif. Your files never touch any server. You can literally unplug your network cable after the page loads and conversion still works. No signup. No limits. No file size caps.

    On HEIC Quick, yes. Your photos never leave your device. Most other "online" converters upload your photos to their servers. Always check whether a converter runs locally (like this one) or server-side.

    Will I lose image quality converting HEIC to JPG?

    JPG is a lossy format, so there is always some compression. HEIC Quick lets you adjust quality from 1 to 100. At 92, the difference is visually imperceptible for almost all photos, while keeping file sizes reasonable.

    Is there a file size or batch limit?

    No artificial limits. The only practical constraint is your device's available memory. Most phones and laptops comfortably handle 50+ photos at once.
